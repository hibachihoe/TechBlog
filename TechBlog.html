Technology Then Vs. Now

An Interview With Bernice Pfluger aka Mom

Bernice Pfluger is my mother and she is 49 years young. She currently works at a lawfirm called Ropes & Gray as a creative designer. She majored in graphic design at FIT. She was one of the first people to work on QuarkXPress in 1987 which is basically a software desktop publisher. She wanted me to include that she likes cute animals and she is single.

Gretchen: How old were you when you got your first computer?

Bernice: Computer. Um. Hmm. I don’t even think I owned by personal computer. I just use the one at work.

Gretchen: What was your favorite piece of technology when you were growing up?

Bernice: The Tiger Electronics football game.

Gretchen: How has technology changed over your lifetime?

Bernice: I think the world is at a much faster pace and information is readily available. But that also means bad people are closer to use more than ever. I want to say that there is bad and good. We have to balance out the bad with good.

Gretchen: Have the changes you’ve seen in technology over your lifetime mostly helped or hurt society?

Bernice: I think it helped society because from now on there will never be an I don’t know to an answer because you can find the answer on the internet.

Gretchen: What is your greatest hope for technology in the future?

Bernice: I hope technology can actually cure diseases and people will come together. That’s what I at least hope it’s like.

Gretchen: What is your greatest fear for technology in the future?

Bernice: That people actually stop communicating in person and they only communicate through technology. We will lose human contact. Also, I hope it doesn’t bring us to world war three.

Gretchen: What advice would you like to share with the younger generation about technology?

Bernice: Always be alert and aware and everything you put out there will stay out there. So think of yourself 20 years from now. Would you like that information to be public?

Gretchen: What is the one thing in technology you wish didn’t exist?

Bernice: I wish all of those photoshopping apps didn’t exist because when let’s say a teenager looks at a photoshopped model or a celebrity they aspire to be that when in reality it’s fake. The goals are unattainable. I think it makes people feel very insecure about themselves. The people putting out these type of images are also sad. I think people fantasise too much with technology and it’s really only fantasy. Now, people are obsessed with their looks.
